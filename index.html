Before you design your own digital solution, it's essential to understand what already exists. By analyzing apps and websites that address similar topics to your project, you'll:

üõ†Ô∏è Understanding Your Tools: FigJam vs Figma

What is Figma?

Figma is a professional design tool where you'll create your actual app or website prototypes later in this project. Think of it as your design workshop where you build polished, detailed interfaces.

What is FigJam?

FigJam is Figma's collaborative whiteboard tool - perfect for brainstorming, organizing ideas, and team collaboration. Think of it as a digital sticky-note board where you can gather information, create diagrams, and work together in real-time.

Feature FigJam (This Activity) Figma (Later Prototyping)
Purpose Brainstorming, research, collaboration Designing actual interfaces
Tools Sticky notes, markers, shapes, text Precise design tools, components, frames
Output Visual boards, mind maps, diagrams High-fidelity prototypes and mockups
Best For Organizing thoughts and findings Creating final designs
üí° Why FigJam for this activity? FigJam makes it easy to collect screenshots, add notes, and organize your competitive analysis visually. Your whole group can work on the same board simultaneously, making collaboration natural and efficient.

üë§ PHASE 1: Individual Analysis

‚è±Ô∏è 1.5 - 2 hours

In this phase, each student works independently to analyze one app or website. You'll document your findings in your individual card on the shared FigJam board.

1 Select Your App/Website 15 min

Choose one app or website that:

  • Relates to the same topic as your group project (e.g., if you're designing for campus dining, analyze a food delivery or restaurant app)
  • Is currently functional and accessible (you need to actually use it)
  • Is different from what your teammates are analyzing
üí° Coordination Tip: Before selecting, quickly check with your team in your group chat to make sure you're not duplicating choices. Analyzing different apps gives your group broader insights.
Example Topic Matches:
  • Campus dining project ‚Üí Analyze: UberEats, Thuisbezorgd, campus restaurant websites
  • Study planning project ‚Üí Analyze: Notion, Todoist, Google Calendar, My Study Life
  • Social events project ‚Üí Analyze: Meetup, Eventbrite, Facebook Events
‚úì Checkpoint - You're ready to move on when:
You've selected one app/website
Your teammates know which one you chose
You can access/use the app or website

2 Explore the App/Website 30-40 min

Now it's time to actually use what you selected. Don't just look at it - interact with it as a real user would.

Your exploration should include:

  • First impression: Open the app/website fresh. What's your immediate reaction? Easy to understand or confusing?
  • Common user task: Try to complete a typical task (e.g., order food, create an event, find information, book something)
  • Navigation: Click around different sections. Can you find things easily? Do you get lost?
  • Key features: Identify the main things this app/website lets you do
‚ö†Ô∏è Take Screenshots: As you explore, capture 4-6 screenshots of important screens or features. You'll need these for your FigJam card. Screenshots should show:
  • The home/landing screen
  • Main navigation or menu
  • A key feature in action
  • Anything that stood out (good or bad)
üí° What to notice while exploring:
  • Can you figure out what to do without instructions? (This relates to intuitive design)
  • Do buttons and links look clickable? (This relates to affordances)
  • Can you tell where you are in the app? (This relates to feedback and visibility)
  • Does it feel easy and pleasant to use? (This relates to overall user experience)

3 Document Your Analysis 45-60 min

Now organize your findings in your individual card on the FigJam board. Use sticky notes, text boxes, and your screenshots to create a visual analysis.

What to include in your card:
Element What to Document Example
App/Website Name & Link Title of your card with the URL "UberEats - ubereats.com"
First Impression Your honest initial reaction when you first opened it "Clean and simple. Immediately showed food near me without needing to search."
Screenshots 4-6 images showing key screens [Add your screenshots with brief captions]
User Flow Observation Describe the steps to complete one main task "To order: (1) See restaurants ‚Üí (2) Pick one ‚Üí (3) Add items to cart ‚Üí (4) Checkout. Only 4 steps, very direct."
Visual Design Notes What do you notice about colors, text size, spacing, images? "Uses lots of food photos. Big, readable text. Lots of white space so it doesn't feel cluttered."
What Works Well List 3-5 strengths (what did you like as a user?) "‚Ä¢ Found what I needed quickly
‚Ä¢ Big buttons, easy to tap
‚Ä¢ Shows delivery time upfront"
What Doesn't Work List 2-4 weaknesses (what frustrated you?) "‚Ä¢ Too many pop-up notifications
‚Ä¢ Hard to filter by dietary needs
‚Ä¢ Checkout had too many steps"
Key Features What are the main things this app/site does? "Order tracking, saved favorites, ratings/reviews, dietary filters"
üí° Writing Tip: Write as if explaining to someone who has never seen this app/website. Be specific - instead of "good navigation," say "I could find the menu in one click from any screen." Use the design vocabulary and concepts that you learned in Block A in in the Design Elements and Principles Lecture when writing your descriptions.
Good vs. Vague Documentation Examples:

‚ùå Vague: "The design is nice and modern."

‚úÖ Specific: "Uses a consistent blue color for all buttons. Clean layout with lots of spacing between elements makes it feel uncluttered."

‚ùå Vague: "Navigation is confusing."

‚úÖ Specific: "I couldn't find the settings menu - I clicked three different icons before finding it. No clear label for where to access my profile."

‚úì Checkpoint - Your individual analysis is complete when:
Your FigJam card has all 8 elements from the table above
You've uploaded 4-6 screenshots with captions
Your observations are specific and clear (not vague)
You've identified both strengths AND weaknesses

üë• PHASE 2: Group Synthesis

‚è±Ô∏è 30-45 minutes

Now work together as a group to compare findings and identify patterns across all the apps/websites you analyzed.

4 Create Your Comparison Matrix 20-25 min

As a group, fill in the comparison table in Section 2 of your FigJam board. This creates a side-by-side view of all analyzed apps.

How to divide the work:

  • One person can create the table structure in FigJam (use shapes or the table tool)
  • Each person fills in the row for their own app based on their individual analysis
  • Review together to ensure consistency in how you're describing things
üí° Collaboration Tip: You can all work on the FigJam board at the same time - you'll see each other's cursors moving! Agree on who fills which row so you don't overlap.
Your comparison matrix should have these columns:
  1. App/Website Name
  2. First Impression (1-2 sentences each)
  3. Ease of Use (Rate: Easy / Medium / Difficult + brief note)
  4. Visual Appeal (Rate: High / Medium / Low + brief note)
  5. Key Features (bullet list)
  6. What Works Well (top 2-3 strengths)
  7. What Doesn't Work (top 2 weaknesses)

5 Discuss and Identify Insights 15-20 min

Now that you can see all apps side-by-side, discuss what you notice as a group. Fill in Section 3 of your FigJam board with these insights.

Discussion Questions to Guide Your Group:
üí° About Common Patterns:
  • What features do most/all of these apps have in common?
  • Do they organize information in similar ways?
  • Are there visual design patterns that appear across multiple apps? (e.g., all use cards, all have bottom navigation, etc.)
üí° About What Users Value:
  • Looking at the "What Works Well" column - what themes do you see? (e.g., speed, simplicity, visual clarity)
  • What features or design choices seemed most important for good user experience?
  • Based on what worked, what do you think users care about most in this type of app?
üí° About Opportunities for Your Design:
  • Looking at the "What Doesn't Work" column - what problems keep appearing?
  • Are there user needs that none of these apps address well?
  • What could your design do differently or better?
  • Are there features you expected to see but didn't find?

Document 3-5 key insights in Section 3, organized under these three headings:

  • Common Patterns We Noticed
  • What Users Seem to Value
  • Gaps & Opportunities for Our Design
Example Insights (for a campus dining project):

Common Patterns: "All apps show food photos prominently. Most use a card-based layout. All include ratings/reviews."

What Users Value: "Speed is crucial - users want to order in under 2 minutes. Clear pricing upfront matters. Real-time tracking reduces anxiety."

Opportunities: "None integrate campus card payment. Dietary filters are weak in all apps. Could add social features for group ordering since students often eat together."

‚úì Checkpoint - Your group synthesis is complete when:
Comparison matrix is filled with all apps
You've documented 3-5 key insights in Section 3
Insights are specific and reference your analysis
You've identified at least 2-3 opportunities for your own design

‚ùì Troubleshooting & FAQ

What if I can't access the app I wanted to analyze?

Choose a different one! Some apps require sign-up or payment - select something freely accessible. Check with your group first to avoid duplicates with their new choices.

How detailed should my screenshots be?

Screenshots should be clear enough that someone who hasn't used the app can understand what you're showing. Include captions that explain what we're looking at.

What if my group members finish at different times?

That's normal! Everyone should complete their individual analysis (Phase 1) before meeting for group work (Phase 2). Use your group chat to coordinate when everyone is ready for the synthesis phase.

Can I analyze a website instead of an app?

Yes! Websites are perfectly fine. The analysis process is the same - you're looking at the digital experience regardless of whether it's on mobile or desktop.

What if I don't understand some of the terms like "user flow"?

Don't worry - we've explained terms in simple language throughout this guide. "User flow" just means the steps someone takes to do something (like the path from opening the app to completing a task). Focus on describing what you observe rather than using perfect terminology.

Our group found similar problems in all the apps - is that okay?

Absolutely! Finding common problems is actually a great insight - it shows you've identified a real opportunity that your design could address. Document this clearly in your "Gaps & Opportunities" section.
